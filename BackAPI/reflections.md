What Worked
The most effective technique was building personas around motivations and beliefs rather than personality adjectives. Early drafts had lines like "Anshuman sir is direct and technical." That produced outputs that sounded like a generic tech bro. Replacing it with "you believe most engineers underperform because of poor structure, not lack of intelligence" gave the model a reason for the directness — and the outputs immediately became more authentic. The persona now had an opinion, not just a tone.
Few-shot examples were the second most impactful element, and more powerful than I expected. The model picks up stylistic patterns — sentence rhythm, where the question lands, how concrete the examples get — almost entirely from the examples embedded in the system prompt. Writing three high-quality examples per persona was the most time-intensive part of the project, but it produced the clearest returns. A system prompt with a weak description but great examples outperformed one with a great description and weak examples every time I tested.
The chain-of-thought instruction also produced a measurable difference. Asking the model to reason step-by-step before composing a response — and specifically what to reason about (emotional subtext for Abhimanyu sir, first-principles logic for Anshuman sir, analogy first for Kshitij sir) — produced responses that felt considered rather than reflexive.
What GIGO Taught Me
GIGO — Garbage In, Garbage Out — was the most immediate and punishing lesson of this project. When I tested early prompts that had lazy descriptions ("You are Abhimanyu Saxena, be helpful and encouraging"), the outputs were embarrassingly generic. The model has no idea who Abhimanyu Saxena is from a name alone. It defaults to "helpful assistant" mode, which produces exactly the kind of content a good persona chatbot should never produce.
Every improvement in output quality could be traced directly to an improvement in input quality. Vague instruction → vague output. Specific belief system → specific voice. Weak examples → the model averages across everything it knows. Strong examples → the model has a concrete template to follow.
The GIGO principle also applies to what you don't specify. Leaving out constraints meant the model would drift — Kshitij sir would give textbook definitions without building intuition, Abhimanyu would make outcome promises he shouldn't make, Anshuman sir would start adding "Great question!" openers. The absence of a constraint is not neutral — it's an invitation for the model's defaults to take over.
What I Would Improve
If I were to iterate on this project, I'd invest in automated evaluation. Right now, quality assessment is entirely subjective — I read the output and judge whether it sounds like the persona. A better approach would be to define measurable criteria (does the response end with a question? does it use the persona's signature phrases? is the length within bounds?) and run the prompt against a test set automatically.
I'd also experiment with injecting dynamic context — for example, telling the model what the user's background is (from an onboarding question) and letting it adapt the advice accordingly. Anshuman sir responding to a Google engineer is a different conversation than Anshuman sir responding to a first-year student, and the current prompts don't capture that nuance.
Finally, I underestimated how much research time was needed. The prompts only became genuinely good after spending time actually watching Kshitij sir's teaching clips, reading Abhimanyu sir's LinkedIn posts, and listening to Anshuman sir's podcast appearances. Research is not a preliminary step — it is the prompt engineering work.
